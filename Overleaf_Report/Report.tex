\section{Introduction}
Question answering systems (QAS) are a central goal of Natural Language Processing, aiming to provide precise answers to user input queries in natural language. Such systems can enable efficient access to large-scale information, significantly reducing the time and effort required compared to a manual search. This is particularly relevant in rapidly expanding, highly specialised domains such as biomedical research, where accessing accurate and relevant information can directly influence critical decisions made by clinicians and researchers. As a result, domain-specific QAS provide a useful tool to support evidence-based decision making by facilitating faster and more reliable information retrieval. \par
In our project, we aim to design and implement a QAS capable of answering biomedical questions using the PubMedQA dataset \cite{jin_pubmedqa_2019}. Our primary objective is to investigate how different text representations and model architectures affect the quality of answers. By systematically comparing alternative approaches, we aim to identify which methods are most effective for handling the challenges of biomedical question answering. \par
In order to evaluate variations of our system we use PubMedQA; a dataset constructed from biomedical research papers. Each instance in the dataset consists of a question derived from a research title, a corresponding abstract (excluding the conclusion) that provides context, a long answer (the abstract conclusion), and a yes/no/maybe short answer (Figure \ref{figure:1}). This structure allows the model architecture to be constructed either as classification or as answer generation, allowing for easy comparison of different AI methods. Furthermore, the inputs are provided as paired question-context instances allowing the task to focus on comprehension and reasoning of the text, instead of retrieval, enabling easier evaluation of model performance. \par

However, biomedical data can provide significant challenges. The language in research papers often contains complex terminology, domain-specific phrases and abbreviations which can be difficult for models to interpret. Additionally, abstracts are usually dense and information-rich, requiring models to identify and extract relevant details from long passages of text. This is highlighted by Figure \ref{figure:subim1} which demonstrates an average input length of 1450 words and some inputs exceeding 2500 words. Many questions also require inferential reasoning rather than simple keyword matching, further increasing difficulty. Our dataset demonstrates a significant label imbalance with 55.2\% of instances belonging in the ‘yes’ class, 33.8\% in the ‘no’ class and only 11\% in the ‘maybe’ class (Figure \ref{figure:subim3}). This distribution indicates that accuracy alone will be an insufficient metric and inspecting macro-average F1 will be required to better understand model performance. These challenges highlight the importance of selecting appropriate text representations and architecture in order to achieve high accuracy and reliability. \par

A key factor influencing QAS performance is text representation. Traditional bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations are simple and easy to interpret but often fail to capture semantic relationships and contextual meaning. In contrast, more advanced embedding-based-methods can represent text in a way that better captures meaning and context which is particularly important in specialised domains. A further key design consideration is model selection. Classification approaches are typically more efficient, and performance is easier to evaluate, especially when answers are limited to predetermined labels. However, they may struggle to capture nuance. In comparison, generative models are more flexible and can produce stronger answer but provide additional challenges such as increased complexity and potential for error. Understanding how text presentation and model architecture interact to affect answer quality is key to optimising performance. \par
A good solution will be able to accurately interpret both the question and context, understand domain-specific semantics, and produce answers that are reliable and consistent.  This project evaluates how different text representations and machine learning methods influence the performance of a biomedical QAS by assessing answer quality. \par


\section{Methods}

\subsection{Baseline System}
The baseline system (Figure \ref{figure:3}) follows a pipeline of six stages. The input, consisting of a question paired with a corresponding abstract, undergoes preprocessing which is handled implicitly by the vectorization step. The preprocessing is handled by the vectorization stage which transforms the text using schikit-learn’s TfidfVectorizer. This is configured with English stop word removal and bi-gram feature extraction which results in tokens that are lowercased, split into unigrams and bigrams and filtered using English stop-words capturing both individual terms and short phrase patterns. This is particularly important in biomedical question answering, where discriminative features often appear in multi-word expressions. The TF-IDF representation assigns weight to terms based on their frequency within a document and their rarity across the corpus. The weight of a term (t) in the document (d) is computed as: 
\[TF–IDF(t, d) = tf(t, d) · log(N / df(t))\]
Where tf( t, d) is the term frequency of t in the document, N is the total number of documents, and df(t) is the number of documents containing t. This yields a high-dimensional sparse vector representation for each input, emphasising discriminate terms while down-weighting common language terms. For prediction, feature vectors representing question-answer pairs are passed into a logistic regression classifier which uses regularised cross-entropy loss. The model estimates the probability of a correct answer using:
\[P(y = c | x) = softmax(Wx + b)\]
The model produces a weight vector that maps sparse TF-IDF features to class probabilities over the three output classes. Model output is evaluated per class using accuracy and macro-averaged F1 score, ensuring that performance is not dominated by the majority class. \par

\subsection{Axes of Experimentation}
In order to evaluate performance and answer quality we systematically experiment with variations in text representation and AI method. We compare sparse text representations with denser embeddings and compare classification methods to answer generation. These two components are most influential in overall system performance and improvements in these components is expected to provide significant performance gains. Text representation determines how effectively meaningful information is captured from the input. Models must reason over long, information-dense contexts which will be particularly challenging for sparse representations. The baseline system using TF-IDF text representation is expected to perform adequately on straightforward instances but will struggle with complex questions and abstracts. TF-IDF representations are not able to capture word order and context which limits their ability to capture nuanced meanings and semantic relationships between words. \par
The second key component of our QA pipeline is the AI method. This dictates how information from the text representation is used to make accurate predictions. The baseline system uses a logistic regression classifier which is chosen for its simplicity, interpretability and effectiveness on high-dimensional sparse data. While it provides a strong and efficient baseline, its performance is limited by its linear nature. This restricts its ability to capture complex patterns and interactions within the data. \par
In order to improve on the limitations of our baseline system we test improvements across the text representation and model axes. The two components are interdependent, as different AI methods require different forms of input data so modifying the model necessitates corresponding changes in the input text representation. We therefore evaluate a set of representative pairings that align with the requirements of each method (Table \ref{table:1}). \par

\subsubsection{Improvement 1: BiomedBERT}
We compare our baseline system using sparse text representation to BERT-based contextual embeddings. Contextual embeddings generated by pretrained biomedical language models BiomedBERT and BioClinical ModernBERT are able to encapsulate context-dependant meanings and are therefore expected to perform better. Multi-phase fine-tuning of BioBERT with long-answer bag-of-word statistics as additional supervision has previously achieved 68.1\% accuracy \cite{jin_pubmedqa_2019}. However this is significantly lower than single-human performance of 78\% accuracy (Figure \ref{table:1}) demonstrating a need for improvement. We select BiomedBERT which is pretrained from scratch using domain-specific biomedical corpora and outperforms continuous pre-training of general models in biomedical NLP tasks. \cite{gu_domain-specific_2021} shows a fully fine-tuned BiomedBERT applied to PubmedQA dataset achieving 55.84\% accuracy. This gives us a performance reference point for our improved frozen encoder + linear classifier strategy. \par

\subsubsection{Improvement 2: BioClinical ModernBERT}
Our next proposed improvement uses a fully neural approach using BioClinical ModernBERT with a sequence-classification head. This model performs text representation and classification jointly with a single end-to-end architecture. The input sequence is processed by the transformer encoder to produce contextualised token embeddings, capturing semantic relationships and domain-specific language usage. Multiple transformer layers, where each token attends to every other token via self-attention allows the model to capture long-range dependencies that cannot be represented through simple lexical overlap or fixed n-gram features. The final representation of the sequence ([CLS] token) is passed to the classification head consisting of a fully connected layer followed by a SoftMax function, which outputs probabilities over the target classes. The prediction is represented by: 
\[P(y = c | x) = SoftMax(W · h_{cls} + b) \]
Where \(h_{cls}\) is the contextual embedding generated by the transformer, and W, and b are the parameters of the classification head. The transformer layers therefore act as a deep feature extractor while the classification head performs the final prediction, allowing the entire system to be optimised together via backpropagation. As a result the system is expected to demonstrate improved performance particularly in cases where correct answer prediction depends on complex relationships, paraphrases or specialised biomedical language \cite{gu_domain-specific_2021, warner_smarter_2025}. \par

\subsubsection{Improvement 3: BioGPT}
Our final proposed improvement is BioGPT. BioGPT is a domain-specific generative Transformer model pre-trained on large-scale biomedical literature, designed to both understand and generate medically relevant natural language. Architecturally, it follows a decoder-only Transformer structure, similar to GPT-style models, enabling it to model text in an autoregressive manner. Due to its specialised pre-training on biomedical corpora, BioGPT is particularly effective at capturing domain specific terminology, relationships, and contextual nuances that are often missed by general language models. \par
BioGPT operates on tokenised text rather than raw strings. Input prompts are processed using the BioGPT tokenizer, which segments text into subword units and maps them to numerical token IDs. These token IDs form the input sequence that the model processes, allowing it to perform computations over continuous vector representations. This tokenisation strategy enables the model to handle complex biomedical vocabulary, including rare or compound terms, while maintaining efficiency. Prior work has demonstrated the effectiveness of this approach for biomedical question answering, with BioGPT achieving 78.2\% accuracy on the PubmedQA dataset \cite{luo_biogpt_2022}, highlighting it’s strong capability in this domain. \par

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\subsection{Experimental Setup}
Models are evaluated using accuracy and macro-average F1 score whereby accuracy measures the proportion of correctly classified instances across all three labels. F1 score is used in addition to accuracy due to uneven class distribution resulting in model bias towards the majority class. F1 score in combination with accuracy therefore more precisely informs of model performance and indicates whether the model is treating each class equally. 
All experiments use the expert-labelled subset of the PubMedQA dataset, PQA-L, which contains 1000 instances. Following the standard protocol the dataset is split into a 500-instance cross-validation split, with the remaining 500 instances reserved for the test set. \par
Our experimental setup starts with a TF-IDF + logistic regression baseline, followed by BiomedBERT embeddings with the same  classifier to isolate the effect of improved representations. The final classification model replaces both components with BioClinical ModernBERT, enabling end-to-end learning. BioMed-R1-8B is used as a fallback for low-confidence, reasoning-intensive cases. Finally, the BioClinical ModernBERT classification model is compared with BioGPT to contrast discriminative and generative approaches. \par
In the baseline experiment, each instance is represented by concatenation of the question and its associated context and mapping categorical labels (‘yes’, ‘no’, ‘maybe’) to numeric classes. Text features are extracted using a TF-IDF vectorizer with English stop-word removal and unigram-bigram representations, fitted on the aggregated 500-instance cross-validation split and applied to the held-out test set. A multinomial logistic regression classifier with balanced class weights and a maximum of 1000 iterations is trained on the complete feature matrix and used to generate predictions and class probabilities for the test data. \par
\subsubsection{Experiment 1}
Our first experiment uses BiomedBERT to encode the question and context separately. BiomedBERT is pre-trained on PubMed abstracts and full-text articles making it well suited to handle the complexities of biomedical language. In our set-up questions are limited to 128 tokens and contexts are limited to 384 tokens, providing a combined total under the 512-token limit of the model. Output of all tokens is average-pooled to obtain a 768-dimensional vector for each input. Four vector combination methods to merge question and context embeddings are tested: 
\begin{itemize}
\item Concat: Concatenation, preserving both representations independently.
\item Diff: Element-wise subtraction, capturing semantic distance between question and context.
\item Prod: Element-wise multiplication, highlighting dimensions where both vectors agree.
\item Concat-Diff: Concatenation of both the original vectors and their difference.
\end{itemize}
All preprocessing is accomplished by the tokenizer of BiomedBERT. Seven parameter configurations were tested: token length allocation (question length: MAX-Q, context length: MAX-C), pooling method, context length limit (CTX-LIMIT), regularisation strength (C), class-weight and batch-size. The best parameter configuration resulted in the following protocol. The questions are truncated to a maximum of 128 tokens (MAX-Q = 128), and the contexts are truncated to a maximum of 384 tokens (MAX-C = 384), ensuring that the total number of tokens for both remains within the 512-token input limit of BiomedBERT. Before tokenisation, each context retains only the first three paragraphs (CTX-LIMIT = 3). This was found to effectively reduce noise introduced by subsequent paragraphs while retaining the most relevant information. Subsequently, the token outputs at all positions are subjected to mean pooling to generate a 768-dimensional sentence vector for each input, rather than relying solely on the representation of the [CLS] token. All embedding features are standardised to zero mean and unit variance using scikit-learn’s StandardScaler before applying a logistic regression classifier with the same settings as baseline (class weight=’balanced’). \par
All experiments were completed on a NVIDIA T4 GPU. Encoding all 10 folds took approximately 130 seconds in total, and training logistic regression on each fold took less than 0.5 seconds. \par
\subsubsection{Experiment 2}
Experiment 2 replaces the external classifier and fixed embedding pipeline of experiment 1 with BioClinical ModernBERT. For this pipeline each instance is encoded as a paired input of question and concatenated contexts. Tokenisation is performed using paired encoding and truncation is applied only to the context to ensure the full question is preserved. Based on analysis of 5000 expert-labelled examples, a maximum sequence length of 512 tokens is selected, covering 98.2\% of cases while maintaining computational efficiency. \par
The model is fine-tuned end-to-end, allowing both representation learning and classification to be optimised together. Initial experiments showed strong class imbalance effects, particularly collapsing on the “maybe” class, so class imbalance is targeted for optimisation. Plain fine-tuning is compared to class-weighted loss with weighted sampling, and weighted sampling alone, using macro-F1 to validate results because accuracy alone masked minority-class failure \cite{younes_handling_2022}. Best performance was achieved by using weighted sampling alone, a learning rate of \(8x10^{-6}\), gradient accumulation of 2, dynamic padding, and early stopping. Additional masked language model fine-tuning on unlabelled data (PQA-U) was explored \cite{gururangan_dont_2020} which improved pretraining loss but did not yield downstream performance gains and was more memory-intensive on the available M1 Max / 32 GB / MPS hardware. \par
To improve performance, a weak-to-strong training strategy was adopted. In stage 1, the encoder is warm started on approximately 200k weakly binary (yes/no) instances. A pilot study on 10\% of the data was used to select the training configuration, which favoured weighted sampling (without class-weighted loss) and a learning rate of \(1 × 10^{-5}\). This configuration was then applied to the full weak dataset, after which the binary classification head was discarded and only the adapted encoder was retained for downstream use. \par
Stage 2 performs fine-tuning on 1000 expert-labelled examples (PQA-L), using the cross-validation split used in previous experiments. The 500-instance cross-validation split is further split into 400 training, 50 calibration, and 50 evaluation instances, explicitly separating model fitting, probability, calibration, and validation. Two pipelines are evaluated. Pipeline S is a fully supervised baseline, consisting of the stage 1 warm-started encoder, a newly initialised 3-class classification head, fine-tuning on the 400 training examples, and temperature scaling on the calibration split to improve probability estimates. Pipeline E extends this with a semi-supervised approach. Two teacher models, initialised with different random seeds, are trained on the same 400 examples and calibrated using temperature scaling. These teachers generate predictions over the unlabelled data, which are combined and filtered using class-specific confidence and margin thresholds. This filtering is stricter for ‘yes’ and ‘no’ than for ‘maybe’ while still requiring agreement between teachers. The resulting soft pseudo-labels are then combined with the labelled data to train a student model, with pseudo-labelled examples down weighted to reflect their lower reliability. \par
Model selection is based on mean macro F1 across folds, with accuracy used only as a secondary metric. The final system is evaluated once on the held-out 500-example test set using a calibrated 10-member soft-voting ensemble, which aggregates predictions while preserving probability information and reducing variance. As a post-selection extension, a selective reasoning fallback was applied on top of the final calibrated ensemble rather than replacing it. Only low-confidence predictions, identified using fixed confidence and margin thresholds, were escalated to BioMed-R1-8B for further processing. If the generated output could not be reliably parsed, the system defaulted to the original prediction. This mechanism was evaluated as an optional heuristic, while the primary reported results are based on the calibrated soft-voting committee. \par
\subsubsection{Experiment 3}
The final experiment evaluates BioGPT as a generative alternative to the classification-based pipelines. Unlike previous experiments, which rely on cross-validation, this setup uses a single train-test split due to the high computational cost of fine-tuning large language models. The full expert-labelled (PQA-L) dataset is used for training, and performance is evaluated on separate held-out test sets, with the same metrics (accuracy and macro F1) applied for consistency, although these may still underrepresent minority-class performance. \par
To enable efficient training, parameter-efficient fine-tuning is performed using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), rather than updating all model parameters. This approach significantly reduces computational cost by training only a small subset of parameters while keeping the majority of the model fixed. Two configurations are explored, with rank values of 8 and 16, corresponding to approximately 0.45\% and 0.90\% of trainable parameters, respectively. A scaling factor of 16 is used to control the contribution of the LoRA updates, and adaptation is applied to key attention modules (query, key, value, and output projections). A dropout rate of 0.05 is included to provide light regularisation. Implementation is carried out using the PEFT library. 
This experiment enables a direct comparison between discriminative model (BioClinical ModernBERT) and generative models (BioGPT), highlighting differences in how each approach handles biomedical question answering. \par

\subsection{Error Analysis and Discussion}
\subsubsection{Baseline}
The results from the baseline system indicate a heavily skewed and weakly discriminative system, achieving moderate overall accuracy (0.514) with low macro F1 (0.309). Performance is dominated by the ‘yes’ class (F1=0.6549 while ‘no’ and ‘maybe’ are substantially weaker (f1=0.274,xyz). Error analysis indicates a systematic bias towards assigning high confidence ‘yes’ labels even for clearly negative or uncertain cases, suggesting that the model is overfitting to lexical or semantic signals correlated with affirmative outcomes rather than capturing true decision boundaries. The highest-confidence errors further highlight poor calibration, as incorrect predictions exceed 0.75 probability, indicating overconfident misclassification. Mislabelling of ‘maybe’ instances as ‘yes’ suggests that the model fails to capture uncertain language, likely due to the limitations of TF-IDF features in representing nuanced scientific discourse. Overall, the baseline is biased and insensitive to minority or ambiguous classes, implying that improvements will require better handling of class imbalance, richer semantic representations, and explicit modelling of uncertainty. \par
\subsubsection{Experiment 1}
The heavily skewed class distribution (\ref{figure:subim2}) results in a trend of models favouring the majority class. Additionally, the small number of maybe instances results in a few misclassifications dramatically reducing per-class recall. 
The best model variation used demonstrates several systematic error patterns. The most prominent error is the misclassification of ‘no’ instances as ‘yes’, indicating that the model struggles to capture negations and instead relies on surface-level lexical similarity. Additionally, the ‘maybe’ class is poorly modelled with most instances being incorrectly assigned, suggesting an inability to detect uncertainty in the language. There is also a clear bias towards predicting ‘yes’ class reflecting overconfidence in positive evidence. Finally, the model exhibits substantial confusion between ‘yes’ and ‘no’ highlighting that the model fails to distinguish between supporting and contradictory evidence, likely due to the limitations of a linear classifier operating on fixed embeddings. \par
Replacing TF-IDF features with BiomedBERT embeddings gives a clear gain in balanced performance (macro-F1: 0.353 to 0.438), mainly by enabling the model to identify some maybe instances and improve no class recognition. The diff combination being the most effective supports the idea that the semantic distance between question and context is a useful signal for this task.
However, several limitations apply. BiomedBERT is frozen, meaning representations are not adapted to this specific classification task, and Logistic Regression can only draw linear decision boundaries in the embedding space. This limits the model’s ability to reason over quantitative content, which the PubMedQA paper identifies as the core difficulty of the task. Additionally, with only around 450 training instances per fold, the classifier is working in a low-data regime. \par
Despite these limitations, BiomedBERT with diff combination consistently outperforms the TF-IDF baseline across all metrics, demonstrating the value of domain-specific contextual representations even in a frozen, non-fine-tuned setting. These findings motivate the use of end-to-end fine-tuning in subsequent experiments. \par
\subsubsection{Experiment 2} 
Earlier experiments narrowed the final BioClinical ModernBERT route before Stage 2. Table 2 shows that the base supervised classifier outperformed the masked-adaptation route on downstream three-class PubMedQA, so masked adaptation was rejected. The weak-label warm-start then provided a strong binary biomedical encoder prior for Stage~2. These results are not directly comparable to the final Stage~2 tables because they were run under different supervision settings and objectives, but they explain why the final evaluation focused on the weak-warm-started classifier family. \par
The main upstream gain therefore came from better route selection and better initialisation, not simply from adding more training stages. Masked adaptation improved language-modelling loss but not downstream QA, whereas weak-label warm-starting gave the encoder a much stronger biomedical yes/no prior at low cost per example. This is why the final Stage~2 comparison was carried out only on the weak-warm-started route. \par
Table 3 compares the two final BioClinical ModernBERT pipelines under the standardised 500-development / 500-test PubMedQA protocol, with outer 10-fold cross-validation on the development half. The simple supervised pipeline achieved the stronger mean macro F1 (0.581 vs  0.578), while the advanced semi-supervised pipeline achieved higher accuracy (0.740 vs 0.712) and higher F1yes (0.821 vs 0.795). This is the central trade-off in our results: pseudo-labelling improved majority-class performance and lifted overall accuracy but did not improve the balanced three-class objective that mattered most for this task. \par
Although the macro-F1 gap is numerically small, it is still meaningful. First, model selection was fixed in advance to favour macro F1, so the comparison is being judged by the metric the system was designed to optimise. Second, the simple pipeline was more stable: its fold-to-fold macro-F1 standard deviation was 0.050, compared with 0.076 for the advanced pipeline. The advanced system had a higher ceiling (its best fold reached 0.702 macro F1, compared with 0.680 for the best simple fold) but it also had a much lower floor. In practice, the pseudo-labelling route could produce strong wins, but not reliably enough across folds to justify replacing the simpler calibrated classifier. \par
The reason for this instability was clear from the pseudo-label selection statistics. In strong folds, the advanced pipeline found enough high-quality candidates in all three classes and filled its intended pseudo pool. In weaker folds, candidate quality collapsed for the minority classes, especially maybe. The clearest example was one fold where the selector found 0 valid ‘no’ candidates and only 26 ‘maybe’ candidates, reducing the pseudo pool from the intended 1,028 examples to 578. This fold then collapsed to 0.0 F1maybe. The important point is that the advanced design itself was reasonable, but its success depended heavily on whether class-aware pseudo-labelling could recover enough clean minority-class signal. When that failed, the student mainly inherited extra confidence on yes rather than better balanced reasoning. \par
This also explains why the advanced pipeline improved accuracy without improving macro F1. The extra pseudo-labels made the model more decisive, especially on examples resembling the dominant yes class, but did not consistently make it better at separating uncertainty from decisiveness. In effect, the advanced system often became a stronger majority-class classifier rather than a better three-class uncertainty-aware classifier. That is why accuracy rose while both F1no and F1maybe fell slightly. \par
Calibration was justified in both pipelines. Learned temperatures were frequently greater than 1 in both the fold-level models and the final committee, indicating that the raw BioClinical ModernBERT probabilities were generally overconfident and needed to be softened before downstream decisions \cite{guo_calibration_2017}. In the simple supervised pipeline, fold-level temperatures ranged from 0.76 to 1.98, and the final committee members ranged from 0.95 to 2.15. This mattered especially in the advanced pipeline, where pseudo-label acceptance depended directly on calibrated confidence and margin thresholds. Calibration improved the trustworthiness of confidence-based decisions, but it did not solve the underlying class asymmetry of the dataset. \par
After applying the pre-registered selection rule, the simple supervised pipeline was taken forward and deployed as a calibrated 10-member soft-voting committee on the untouched 500-example test set. Table 4 gives the final official result. The selected system reached 0.702 accuracy and 0.556 macro F1. Relative to the development-side mean of 0.581 macro F1, this is a modest but expected drop under stricter unseen evaluation rather than a collapse in generalisation, suggesting that the selected pipeline transferred reasonably well but remained bottlenecked by the same minority-class weakness. \par
The error analysis shows a highly consistent pattern. On the official test set, the final model correctly classified 120/169 no examples and 223/276 yes examples, but only 8/55 maybe examples. Most of the remaining maybe cases were pushed into yes (31) or no (16). The key empirical insight from the full BioClinical ModernBERT pipeline is therefore not that the model fails broadly, but that it still struggles to preserve ambiguity once evidence becomes even moderately directional. In application terms, the system is strongest on clear biomedical conclusions and weakest on abstracts whose evidence is mixed, inconclusive, or only partially supportive. This suggests that further gains are more likely to come from better uncertainty modelling and stronger minority-class supervision than from simply increasing model complexity or adding more pseudo-labels. \par
The final post-selection BioMed-R1-8B fallback was useful as a diagnostic but did not change the overall conclusion. Selective reasoning on only the most uncertain committee cases added complexity and cost but did not improve the deployment choice enough to replace the calibrated committee. Overall, the results support three conclusions. First, weak-label warm-starting was valuable and was the correct upstream choice. Second, class-aware pseudo-labelling was promising but not robust enough to outperform the simpler pipeline under the standardised protocol. Third, the main remaining bottleneck is not overall classification capacity but minority-class uncertainty modelling, especially on maybe examples. \par
\subsubsection{Experiment 3}
Error analysis of the BioGPT experiment highlights several factors influencing performance. Strong class imbalance of the PubmedQA dataset is addressed through upsampling of the ‘no’ and ‘maybe’ classes. This was found to improve class representation but introduced duplicated samples that may increase the risk of overfitting. Second, there is an inherent mismatch between the generative nature of BioGPT and the classification objective of the task. Initial fine-tuning produced ambiguous free-text outputs, and although constrained prompting was used to restrict responses to ‘yes’, ‘no’, or ‘maybe’, inconsistencies remained. The model was also highly sensitive to prompt design; explicitly instructing model to output ‘maybe’ for weak or contradictory evidence significantly reduced accuracy by biasing predictions towards this class. Additionally, the relatively small size of the labelled dataset limits the model’s ability to learn robust decision boundaries. Input length constraints (maximum 512 tokens) further restrict performance, as longer contexts must be truncated, potentially removing important information. Finally, the use of parameter-efficient fine-tuning via LoRA, while computationally efficient, limits the extent to which the model can adapt compared to full fine-tuning, which has been shown to yield stronger performance in prior work \cite{luo_biogpt_2022}. \par
